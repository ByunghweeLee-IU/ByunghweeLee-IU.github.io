About Me

I am a postdoctoral research fellow at Indiana University Bloomington, specializing in cultural analytics, digital art history, and computational social science. I earned my Ph.D. in physics from the Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST). During my time at KAIST, I developed a robust interdisciplinary career, examining society, culture, and art through the lens of statistical physics and complex network science. My seminal work, published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS), employed information theory to analyze the evolution of landscape art compositions. This study gained recognition in major media outlets, including Forbes and Cosmos magazine, and was featured in a PNAS commentary, underscoring the integration of complex scientific methods with extensive art historical data.

Currently, my research utilizes large language models (LLMs) to examine complex human behaviors on digital platforms. My recent studies employ LLMs to represent the intricate relationships between various human beliefs. These belief embeddings help predict an individual's new beliefs and uncover underlying mechanisms in belief formation. My findings demonstrate how LLMs, combined with aggregated online data, can illuminate the processes of human belief formation and decision-making.

I am open to collaborations and can be reached at bl46@iu.edu.
