Emergence of Stereotypes and Affective Polarization from Belief Network Dynamics

2026-04-21

Emergence of Stereotypes and Affective Polarization from Belief Network Dynamics#

One-sentence summary. An agent-based belief-network model shows that two minimal ingredients — social influence and a drive for internal coherence — are sufficient to spontaneously generate stereotypes and strong affective polarization, even in the absence of any underlying ideological disagreement or network homophily.

1. Core Questions#

  • Q1. How can seemingly unrelated beliefs become associated with each other (i.e., how do stereotypes emerge) in the absence of any underlying causal or factual link?
  • Q2. How do such newly-formed belief associations give rise to affective polarization — ingroup favoritism and outgroup animosity — even when ideological conflict is absent?

2. Motivation & Gap#

  • Polarization is usually attributed to ideological disagreement, motivated reasoning, homophily, or echo chambers. Yet classic minimal-group experiments show arbitrary categorization is enough to spark bias, and empirically “affective polarization often exceeds substantive disagreement.”
  • Existing opinion-dynamics models treat opinions as isolated scalars, ignoring that beliefs are interconnected networks mutually constraining each other.
  • Prior belief-network models (e.g., Rodriguez et al. 2016; Aiyappa, Flammini & Ahn 2024) updated only directly communicated beliefs; they did not allow endogenous, coherence-driven updates to related beliefs after a social interaction.
  • Gap: no mechanistic account of how arbitrary associations crystallize into group-level stereotypes and then cascade into affective polarization, without homophily or ideological disagreement.

3. Key Contributions#

  1. An extended belief-network agent-based model combining (i) social influence on directly communicated beliefs and (ii) endogenous coherence-driven updates on related beliefs, operationalized via two-step weighted random walks over the belief graph.
  2. Demonstrates that stereotypes arise spontaneously around a neutral concept with no underlying reality: group identity alone plus coherence pressure is sufficient. This reframes stereotypes as an emergent property of belief organization under uncertainty rather than a response to real group differences.
  3. Shows affective polarization emerges from stereotyping — strong ingroup favoritism (+1) and outgroup derogation (−1) arise without any initial knowledge of others’ group affiliations and without ideological conflict.
  4. Rules out structural explanations: polarization emerges in a well-mixed network without homophily or community structure — purely from cognitive dynamics.
  5. Parameter-space analysis (α, β) identifies the regime where polarization arises: substantial social influence (α > 0.5) combined with any non-zero coherence pressure (β > 0). Notably, very strong β reduces polarization because beliefs crystallize before they can align with group identity.

4. Background & Prior Work#

  • Social Balance / Heider Theory (Heider 1946): triads are stable when the product of signs is positive; used to formalize internal coherence.
  • Cognitive Dissonance (Festinger 1957): motivational grounding for the coherence-seeking drive.
  • Belief network tradition — Converse 1964; Martin 2002; Friedkin et al. 2016; Vlasceanu et al. 2024 (bending model); Rodriguez, Bollen & Ahn 2016; Aiyappa, Flammini & Ahn 2024 (weighted belief networks, simple/complex contagion).
  • Associative diffusion (Goldberg & Stein 2018) and opinion cascades (Macy et al. 2019) — stochastic emergence of associations.
  • Minimal group paradigm (Tajfel et al. 1971) — arbitrary categories suffice for intergroup bias.
  • Illusory correlations & false consensus (Ross et al. 1977) — cognitive biases that map onto the observed minority who “misperceive” group membership.

5. Method / Model#

  • Approach type: Agent-based simulation on a two-layer structure — a social network of agents, each carrying an internal belief network.
  • Core setup:
  • Social graph G = (V, E), N = 100 agents, M = 200 random edges.
  • Each agent i has a belief network B_i with concept nodes C = {self, latte, Group A, Group B, other agents…} and signed, weighted edges b_i(c_x, c_y) ∈ [−1, +1].
  • Each agent is affiliated with Group A or Group B; the self→own-group edge is fixed at +1 and self→other-group at −1.
  • All other beliefs initialized near zero: N(μ=0, σ=10⁻⁵).
  • Key equations / algorithms:
  • Triad energy (Eq. 1): E_i(c_x, c_y, c_z) = −b_i(c_x,c_y)·b_i(c_x,c_z)·b_i(c_y,c_z)
  • Internal dissonance (Eq. 2): d(B_i) = (1/|T|) Σ E_i over all triads.
  • Update rule (Eqs. 3–4): b_i ← b_i + f (social influence) + g (coherence update on an adjacent edge).
  • Social influence (Eq. 5): f ~ N(α·(b_j − b_i), σ) — receiver’s belief drifts toward the sender’s.
  • Endogenous update (Eq. 6): g ~ N(−β·∂d(B_i)/∂b_i(c_x,c_y), σ) — gradient descent on dissonance.
  • The adjacent edge for coherence update is chosen via two-step weighted random walks starting from one of the two concepts involved in the just-updated belief; destination probability ∝ number of walks terminating there.
  • Beliefs clipped to [−1, +1] (Eq. 7).
  • Parameters & assumptions:
  • α = 1 (social influence), β = 1 (coherence), σ = 0.1 (noise). Swept in [0, 3] for sensitivity.
  • Fixed group identity; well-mixed population; no homophily; agents cannot verify beliefs against reality; no distinction between beliefs and meta-beliefs.
  • Baselines / comparisons: Parameter sweeps with α = 0 (no social influence) and β = 0 (no coherence) serve as null conditions. Alternative reinforcement-style social-influence term also tested; results qualitatively identical.

6. Dataset / Experimental Setup#

  • Source: Pure simulation — no empirical dataset. Custom code in Julia (model) + Python (analysis).
  • Size / scale: N = 100 agents, M = 200 edges, 2.5 × 10⁶ time steps per run to reach steady state; each cell in parameter sweeps averaged over 10 runs.
  • Preprocessing: None — synthetic initializations.
  • Ground truth / labels: None (neutral concept “latte” has no true association with either group).
  • Code/replication: https://github.com/rachithaiyappa/emerging_beliefs
  • Limitations of the data: Simulation-only; no empirical validation in this paper (authors mark this as future work: controlled belief-updating experiments and observational studies of association spread).

7. Key Results#

  • Stereotype emergence (Fig. 3): Starting from near-zero beliefs about latte, the population ends with strong, group-aligned associations (Group A → likes latte; Group B → dislikes latte). Opinion polarization P_O rises from 0 → ~2 (max).
  • Affective polarization emergence (Fig. 4): Without any initial knowledge of others’ group affiliation, agents converge on +1 valence toward ingroup and −1 toward outgroup. P_A rises from 0 → ~2.
  • Internal dissonance drops from 0 to ≈ −1 (Fig. 3h) as populations settle into coherent belief systems — polarization is stabilized by coherence.
  • Phase diagram (Fig. 5): Polarization appears only for α > 0.5 and β > 0. Very high β suppresses polarization because beliefs crystallize before they can learn group associations.
  • False-consensus-like minority: A small fraction of agents flip the sign, forming positive beliefs about outgroup neighbors they (incorrectly) perceive as ingroup — a mechanism consistent with the false-consensus effect (Ross et al. 1977).
  • Homophily is not required: all of the above holds on a well-mixed random network.

9. Interpretation & Implications#

  • Reframes polarization: not an outcome of disagreement over values, but an emergent property of how humans organize beliefs under uncertainty.
  • Provides a cognitively grounded alternative to homophily-based models of opinion dynamics.
  • Offers a candidate mechanism for the COVID-19 vaccine polarization pattern: pre-pandemic neutrality → coherence-driven alignment around political identity via prominent ingroup signals and associative chains (lockdowns ↔ freedom ↔ mandates).
  • Because the mechanism is domain-agnostic, the same dynamics could underlie polarization in politics, climate, science denial, and culture.

10. Limitations#

  • Author-acknowledged: 1. Well-mixed network — real networks have structure that could amplify dynamics. 2. Only two groups; real identities overlap. 3. Group identity fixed; real identities are fluid. 4. Only one neutral concept tracked. 5. Agents cannot verify beliefs against reality. 6. Random transmission — no selective exposure or attention. 7. All agents have equal influence — no opinion leaders or media asymmetry. 8. No distinction between beliefs and meta-beliefs (beliefs about others’ beliefs); theory of mind is absent.
  • My own reading:
  • No empirical calibration of α, β; the “COVID vaccine” illustration is narrative, not quantitative.
  • Population small (N=100); robustness to network size / density could be examined more systematically.
  • The two-step random-walk operationalization of “adjacency” is a modeling choice with its own implications (longer walks would likely change spread dynamics).

11. Open Questions & Future Work#

  • Empirically test the prediction that exposure to a few ingroup members’ behavior suffices to seed a group-level stereotype (controlled experiments or observational studies of association spread).
  • Add community structure / homophily — does it amplify or merely accelerate?
  • Multiple overlapping identities and fluid group membership.
  • Multiple interacting neutral concepts — do stereotypes cluster?
  • Introduce theory of mind / second-order beliefs to separate “what I believe” from “what I think others believe.”
  • Asymmetric influence and selective exposure.

13. Personal Takeaways#

  • The single idea to carry forward: polarization and stereotypes can be emergent properties of coherence-seeking in belief networks — you don’t need echo chambers, disagreement, or even knowledge of others’ identities. Cognitive structure alone can produce the whole pattern.
  • Methodologically, the two-step-random-walk operationalization of “related belief” is a clean, reusable primitive for endogenous belief updates in any belief-network model.

15. Glossary#

  • Belief network (B_i): An individual’s signed, weighted graph of concept–concept associations; edge weight b_i(c_x, c_y) ∈ [−1, +1] encodes the perceived association.
  • Social Balance Theory: A triad is stable when the product of its three edge signs is positive (e.g., “friend of friend is friend”); unstable otherwise.
  • Internal dissonance d(B_i): Average triad energy across the belief network; lower = more coherent.
  • Social influence (α): Strength with which a receiver’s belief drifts toward a sender’s.
  • Internal coherence pressure (β): Strength of the gradient-descent step reducing d(B_i).
  • Endogenous update: Coherence-driven update on a related belief, triggered by (but not directly communicated in) a social interaction.
  • Opinion polarization P_O(c): Absolute difference between Group A and Group B mean opinions toward concept c; range [0, 2].
  • Affective polarization P_A: Mean (ingroup valence − outgroup valence) across the population; range [0, 2].
  • False consensus effect: Tendency to overestimate the extent to which others share one’s beliefs; appears in the model as a minority who project their own alignment onto neighbors.
